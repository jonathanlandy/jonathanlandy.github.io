How Return Rates Affect Margin in E-commerce

Published on Mon 21 July 2025

E-commerce apparel return rates today average around 20% — but this average is rising, and rates on individual products vary widely. In this post, we introduce simple equations to estimate an item’s expected profit and margin based on its return rate, damage rate, and price.

Inventory managers can use these formulas to assess true profitability across their assortment over time. For example, when the return rate hits 40% — a realistic figure for a hard-to-size item — the margin can fall to half of its ideal level.

Expected profit and margin formulas

We assume the item has the following characteristics:

  • \(r\) = return rate (item is returned but not damaged)
  • \(d\) = damage rate (item is returned or lost in a way that requires a full write-off)
  • \(AUC\) = average unit cost to the retailer
  • \(p\) = average retail price
  • \(O\) = operations cost per shipment (shipping + logistics, etc.)

From these, we derive the following formula (see appendix for derivation):

\begin{equation} \text{expected profit per item} = p - AUC - \frac{O}{1 - r} - \frac{p \cdot d}{1-r}. \tag{1} \end{equation}

The first two terms reflect the “ideal” profit – price minus unit cost. The third term accounts for average operational expenses, and the fourth for expected losses due to damage. This last is an approximation that holds well when \(d \lesssim 10\%\) – a condition that typically holds in practice.

To compute expected margin, we have to divide (1) by the expected revenue per item. This gives,

\begin{equation} \text{expected margin} = 1 - (\frac{AUC + \frac{O}{1 - r}}{p}) \cdot (1 + \frac{ d}{1-r}). \tag{2} \end{equation}

Both (1) and (2) decrease with \(r\)and\(d\).

Example

Consider an item with \(r = 20\%\), \(d = 2\%\), \(AUC\) = $15, \(p\) = $50, and \(O\) = $9. The ideal margin – based solely on cost vs price – is

\begin{equation} \text{ideal margin} = \frac{50 - 15}{50} = 70\%. \tag{3} \end{equation}

But using formula (2), the actual margin drops to

\begin{equation} \text{actual margin} = 46\%. \tag{4} \end{equation}

If returns rise to \(40\%\) (perhaps the garment sizing is hard to judge online), margin drops further

\begin{equation} \text{"high return rate" margin} = 38\%. \tag{5} \end{equation}

If the damage rate is also increased to \(d = 5\%\), margin goes to

\begin{equation} \text{"high return \& damage rates" margin} = 35\%, \tag{6} \end{equation}

half the ideal (3).

A company with $10 million in annual revenue would have $7 million to cover HQ costs under margin (3), $4.6 million under margin (4), $3.8 million under margin (5), and just $3.5 million under margin (6). We conclude that working to keep return and damage rates down can pay off meaningfully for e-commerce brands.

Appendix: Derivations

With return-without-damage rate \(r\), the expected number of shipments needed to sell an item is

\begin{equation} \text{"expected shipments"} \sim \frac{1}{1-r} \tag{7} \end{equation}

With a cost of \(O\) per shipment, total expected operational costs are then

\begin{equation} \text{"expected ops cost"} \sim \frac{O}{1-r} \tag{8} \end{equation}

If damage occurs independently with probability \(d\) per shipment, and damage is rare (\(d \lesssim 10\%\)), then the expected loss to damage is approximately:

\begin{equation} \text{"expected damage loss"} \sim \frac{p \cdot d}{1-r} \tag{9} \end{equation}

Here, we assume that an item that is damaged cannot be sold, and so we lose the sale price \(p\) when this happens. Putting these together with the basic price-cost difference yields the expected profit formula (1).

To get to margin, we have to divide (1) by the expected revenue per item. This is

\begin{equation} \text{"expected revenue"} \sim p - \frac{p \cdot d}{1 - r} \tag{10} \end{equation}

Doing this and keeping results only up to first order in \(d\) gives (2).
